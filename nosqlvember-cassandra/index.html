Introduction to NoSQL

What NoSQL Is

  • Really could be Non-relational
  • Optimized for specific use cases
    • Caching
    • High Availability
    • Document Storage
    • Searching
    • Graphs
  • All about access patterns
  • NoSQL is a bit of a mis-nomer. NoRDBMS would be a better name.
  • Typically a NoSQL DB is optimized for a specific use case or access pattern.
  • You don't model your data in NoSQL DBs, you analyze your data access patterns and then see how you can optimize them with a NoSQL DB.

    • What NoSQL Isn't

    • Not a replacement for relational databases
    • A cure for all your scaling problems
    • A savings measure over a RDBMS
  • Don't throw out your RDBMS tool of choice. It is still a very powerful tool.
  • Replacing your RDBMS with a NoSQL will not magically make your app "Web Scale". Take your pain points and analyze your options.
  • NoSQL databases come with a hidden cost: the knowledge and expertise needed to install and manage production environments.

    • NoSQL Takeaways

    • Lots of options
    • Choose the right tool for the job
    • It is all about access patterns, not "what is cool"
    • Put another way: Model your queries, not your data.
    • VT has lots of great people knowledgeable on NoSQL!

    Cassandra

    What is Cassandra?

    • Open Source
    • Best of Big Table and Dynamo
    • Columns & Column Families (cf/row-key/column => value)
    • AP - w/ Tunable CAP
    • Linear Scaling
    • Apache Foundation Project
    • Data structure of Google's Big Table - Think distributed hash table.
    • Billions of rows, millions of columns
    • AP (Eventual Consistency) - Expect failure, it is the norm.
    • Tune # of replicas, and then each request can
    • Linear scaling, single qeury performance takes the same on 6 nodes or 100 nodes. Throughput goes up with # of nodes.

    What it is good for?

    • Write heavy workloads
    • Sparse Data
    • Data Analysis
    • Log Analysis
    • Time series data
    • CQL - Makes working with data familiar
    • Cassandra handles writes incredibly well. Any node can accept any value for writing, and ensure it is saved (to the required number of replicas).
    • Sparse data (each row may have different columns).
    • The efficiency of wide row (many columns) make cassandra great for storing values over time.
    • CQL lets you write very SQL like queries.

    What isn't it good for?

    • Read heavy workloads
    • Joins/relational data
    • Queries comparing values (besides equality)
    • For read heavy workloads, HBase is CP with faster read queries.
    • Complex queries are difficult in cassandra. If your access patterns require a lot of conditionals, look elsewhere.

    Cassandra Takeaways

    • Giant distributed hash table with tunable consistency.
    • Great for write heavy, high availability analytical application.
    • Bad for complex queries or read heavy workloads.
